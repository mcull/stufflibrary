StuffLibrary is not a new idea. Neighbors have been lending each other things for as long as there have been neighbors, and people have been thinking carefully about _why that works_ — and what breaks it — for a very long time. Commons scholars, mutual aid organizers, gift economy anthropologists, tool librarians, and a lot of grandmothers all got here first.

What's below is our attempt to be honest about that. It's part genealogy — these are the shoulders we're standing on — and part invitation: if borrowing a ladder from the neighborhood library scratched some itch you can't quite name, one of these books probably names it.

We haven't read all of these cover to cover, and we won't pretend otherwise. But these are the landmarks of the territory StuffLibrary lives in, chosen because they're significant and readable rather than obscure. Better yet: check them out from your public library. It would be weird to buy them.

Works marked **⭐ start here** are the friendliest doors in.

## 1. The commons & its governance

**"The Tragedy of the Commons" — Garrett Hardin (1968)**
The famous _Science_ essay that claimed shared resources are doomed: everyone grazes their cattle, nobody restrains themselves, the pasture dies. It's here as the foil — the argument an entire generation of researchers, led by Elinor Ostrom, spent decades proving wrong with real-world evidence. Every neighborhood that keeps a shared ladder in circulation without a corporation or a cop involved is a small counterexample. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Tragedy%20of%20the%20Commons%22%20au%3AHardin).)_

**Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action — Elinor Ostrom (1990)**
Ostrom went out and studied actual commons — Swiss alpine pastures, Spanish irrigation canals, Japanese village forests — and found ordinary people governing shared resources successfully for centuries. Her design principles (clear membership, rules made by the people they bind, cheap conflict resolution, graduated consequences) are quietly the operating manual for any lending community. She won the Nobel in economics for this; the tragedy of the commons turned out to be optional. _(See also [our page on how StuffLibrary builds on her research](/why-this-works).)_ _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Governing%20the%20Commons%22%20au%3AOstrom).)_

**Think Like a Commoner: A Short Introduction to the Life of the Commons — David Bollier (2014)** ⭐ start here
The most readable single introduction to the commons: what they are, how enclosure took them from us, and how people keep rebuilding them anyway. Bollier writes for neighbors, not seminar rooms. If you want one short book explaining why a street full of shared tools is part of something ancient and worldwide, it's this one. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Think%20Like%20a%20Commoner%22%20au%3ABollier).)_

**The Wealth of Networks: How Social Production Transforms Markets and Freedom — Yochai Benkler (2006)**
Benkler explained why strangers on the internet built Wikipedia and Linux for free: given the right infrastructure, people cooperate at scale outside both the market and the state. He named "commons-based peer production," which is exactly the license an open-source lending platform runs on. The insight travels well from software to stand mixers. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Wealth%20of%20Networks%22%20au%3ABenkler).)_

**Free, Fair and Alive: The Insurgent Power of the Commons — David Bollier & Silke Helfrich (2019)**
The deeper follow-up to _Think Like a Commoner_: commoning is a verb, a living social practice, not a pile of stuff. Bollier and Helfrich catalog the patterns — ritual, reciprocity, shared stewardship — that keep commons alive across cultures. Useful reading for anyone who suspects the hard part of a stuff library isn't the software but the relationships, because it is. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Free%2C%20Fair%20and%20Alive%22%20au%3ABollier).)_

## 2. Mutual aid & solidarity

**Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution — Peter Kropotkin (1902)**
The anarchist naturalist's answer to "survival of the fittest": across species and across human history — medieval guilds, village commons, neighborly custom — cooperation, not just competition, is what gets creatures through. More than a century old and it still reads like a defense of borrowing a neighbor's shovel. The root text for everything called "mutual aid" since. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Mutual%20Aid%22%20au%3AKropotkin).)_

**A Paradise Built in Hell: The Extraordinary Communities That Arise in Disaster — Rebecca Solnit (2009)** ⭐ start here
When earthquakes and hurricanes hit, people don't turn on each other — they improvise soup kitchens, share what they have, and often describe those weeks as the most alive of their lives. Solnit's argument is that the generous neighbor is who we actually are, and everyday institutions just fail to ask anything of that self. A lending library is a way of asking without waiting for the disaster. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22A%20Paradise%20Built%20in%20Hell%22%20au%3ASolnit).)_

**Collective Courage: A History of African American Cooperative Economic Thought and Practice — Jessica Gordon Nembhard (2014)**
A recovered history of Black communities in America pooling resources — mutual insurance societies, buying clubs, credit unions, co-op farms — often in the teeth of violent opposition. It's a reminder that sharing infrastructure has always been survival strategy and political practice, not lifestyle experiment. Anyone building neighborhood sharing tools should know whose tradition they're standing in. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Collective%20Courage%22%20au%3ANembhard).)_

**Emergent Strategy: Shaping Change, Changing Worlds — adrienne maree brown (2017)**
A field guide to how small, humble, relationship-scale actions compound into large change, drawing on natural systems and Octavia Butler. brown's case for "small is all" — that the pattern of the whole lives in the tiniest interactions — is encouragement for anyone whose revolution currently looks like lending a stand mixer three doors down. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Emergent%20Strategy%22%20au%3Abrown).)_

**Mutual Aid: Building Solidarity During This Crisis (and the Next) — Dean Spade (2020)** ⭐ start here
A short, intensely practical handbook from the COVID era: what mutual aid is, how it differs from charity (no means tests, no saviors, solidarity not pity), and how groups actually run — meetings, burnout, money, conflict. If your neighborhood sharing project ever needs to become an organization, this is the pamphlet to pass around first. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Mutual%20Aid%22%20au%3ASpade).)_

## 3. Gift economies & reciprocity

**The Gift (Essai sur le don) — Marcel Mauss (1925)**
The anthropology classic that found, across Polynesia, the Pacific Northwest, and archaic Europe, that gifts are never really free — they create obligation, relationship, and social fabric on purpose. That's the point: an object given carries the giver with it. Every borrowed drill that comes back with a plate of cookies is behaving exactly as Mauss described. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Gift%20%28Essai%20sur%20le%20don%29%22%20au%3AMauss).)_

**Stone Age Economics — Marshall Sahlins (1972)**
Home of "the original affluent society": hunter-gatherers worked a few hours a day and wanted for little, because affluence can mean low needs rather than high production. Sahlins showed that scarcity is often manufactured by the economy that claims to cure it. A neighborhood where nobody needs to own a rarely-used tool is affluent in precisely his sense. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Stone%20Age%20Economics%22%20au%3ASahlins).)_

**The Gift: Imagination and the Erotic Life of Property — Lewis Hyde (1983)**
Hyde's beloved meditation on why some things — art, talent, and arguably a good ladder — lose their spirit when hoarded and gain it when kept in motion. His central image is the gift that must circulate to stay alive. It's the most beautiful available answer to "why not just let everyone buy their own." _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Gift%22%20au%3AHyde).)_

**Debt: The First 5,000 Years — David Graeber (2011)**
Graeber's sprawling history demolishes the myth that money rescued us from barter; what actually came first were webs of credit, favor, and "everyday communism" — from each according to ability, to each according to need, among people who know each other. Neighbors keeping loose, unwritten accounts of borrowed things aren't primitive economics; they're the original kind. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Debt%22%20au%3AGraeber).)_

**Sacred Economics: Money, Gift, and Society in the Age of Transition — Charles Eisenstein (2011)**
A heartfelt argument that money as we know it manufactures scarcity and dissolves community, and that gift relationships do the opposite. Eisenstein is the movement voice many people met these ideas through. Read it for the animating spirit: every act of sharing is a small secession from the scarcity story. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Sacred%20Economics%22%20au%3AEisenstein).)_

**Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge and the Teachings of Plants — Robin Wall Kimmerer (2013)**
A Potawatomi botanist braids Indigenous teaching and Western science into essays about living in reciprocity with a generous world. Her "Honorable Harvest" — take only what you need, use it well, be grateful, give back — is a complete ethics for a lending library in four lines. Millions of readers found this book; it changes how you look at everything you own. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Braiding%20Sweetgrass%22%20au%3AKimmerer).)_

**The Serviceberry: Abundance and Reciprocity in the Natural World — Robin Wall Kimmerer (2024)** ⭐ start here
A small, luminous book that asks: what if we ran our economies the way a serviceberry tree runs its June abundance — wealth measured by what flows through you to others? Kimmerer names Little Free Libraries and tool shares directly as gift economies hiding in plain sight. It is, almost literally, the mission statement of a neighborhood stuff library; read it in an afternoon. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Serviceberry%22%20au%3AKimmerer).)_

## 4. Enough: consumption critique, degrowth & the circular economy

**The Waste Makers — Vance Packard (1960)**
The bestseller that blew the whistle on planned obsolescence — products designed to break, styles designed to shame you into replacing them — back when the strategy was new. Sixty-plus years on, it reads less like history than diagnosis. Sharing durable things is the quiet refusal Packard was hoping someone would organize. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Waste%20Makers%22%20au%3APackard).)_

**Small Is Beautiful: A Study of Economics As If People Mattered — E. F. Schumacher (1973)**
Schumacher's essays argue for human-scale economies and technology appropriate to communities rather than empires, and for treating "enough" as an achievement rather than a failure. His case that we should maximize well-being with minimum consumption is the arithmetic of every shared drill. The subtitle alone is worth the price. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Small%20Is%20Beautiful%22%20au%3ASchumacher).)_

**Tools for Conviviality — Ivan Illich (1973)**
Illich divided tools into two kinds: convivial ones that expand what ordinary people can do together, and industrial ones that make us dependent clients of systems we can't control. He wanted communities to own and govern their own means. A neighborhood tool library run on open-source software is about as convivial — in his exact sense — as tools get. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Tools%20for%20Conviviality%22%20au%3AIllich).)_

**The Overspent American: Upscaling, Downshifting, and the New Consumer — Juliet Schor (1998)**
Schor showed that Americans no longer keep up with the neighbors — we keep up with television and (now) feeds, aspiring to reference groups far richer than ourselves, and going into debt for it. Her research on "downshifters" who traded stuff for time is an early portrait of the people lending libraries serve. She reappears in the last section, still asking who sharing actually benefits. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Overspent%20American%22%20au%3ASchor).)_

**Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things — William McDonough & Michael Braungart (2002)**
An architect and a chemist argue that waste is a design failure: in nature, everything is food for something else, and our things should be made to circulate the same way. It's the design-world root of the circular economy. Lending is circularity you can do this weekend, without waiting for industry to redesign anything. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Cradle%20to%20Cradle%22%20au%3AMcDonough).)_

**The Story of Stuff — Annie Leonard (2010; animated film 2007)**
The twenty-minute film seen by tens of millions, expanded into a book: where our stuff comes from, what it costs people and places we never see, and where it goes when we tire of it. Leonard's punchline is that the system, not the shopper, is the problem — and that shared, durable, repairable things are part of the fix. The gateway drug for consumption critique. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Story%20of%20Stuff%22%20au%3ALeonard).)_

**Towards the Circular Economy — Ellen MacArthur Foundation (report series, 2012–2014)**
The reports that carried "circular economy" from fringe idea to boardrooms and city halls, with hard numbers on the trillions wasted in take-make-dispose. Notably, they flagged access-over-ownership — sharing, leasing, product-as-service — as core circular strategies. When your stuff library needs to persuade a grant committee, this is the citation. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Towards%20the%20Circular%20Economy%22%20au%3AFoundation).)_

**Doughnut Economics: Seven Ways to Think Like a 21st-Century Economist — Kate Raworth (2017)** ⭐ start here
Raworth redrew the goal of economics as a doughnut: meet everyone's needs (the inner ring) without breaking the planet (the outer ring), and stop worshiping growth in between. She names household and commons provisioning as real economy, not rounding error. The friendliest big-picture book on this list — and neighborhood sharing lives right in the sweet part of her doughnut. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Doughnut%20Economics%22%20au%3ARaworth).)_

**Less Is More: How Degrowth Will Save the World — Jason Hickel (2020)**
The clearest popular case for degrowth: rich economies can't decarbonize fast enough while chasing endless growth, so we should shrink what's wasteful and expand what's shared — public wealth, longer-lasting goods, more free time. Hickel's premise that private riches can shrink while lives get better is what every tool library demonstrates in miniature. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Less%20Is%20More%22%20au%3AHickel).)_

## 5. Social infrastructure & neighborliness

**The Death and Life of Great American Cities — Jane Jacobs (1961)**
Jacobs watched actual sidewalks and found that neighborhood safety and trust are produced by countless small public contacts — the nod, the favor, the borrowed key held for a neighbor. She called it a "web of public respect and trust," a resource no institution can fake. Every loan of a ladder is one more strand in the web she described. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Death%20and%20Life%20of%20Great%20American%20Cities%22%20au%3AJacobs).)_

**A Pattern Language: Towns, Buildings, Construction — Christopher Alexander, Sara Ishikawa & Murray Silverstein (1977)**
A strange, generous catalog of 253 design patterns for making places that feel alive, from "Identifiable Neighborhood" to "Common Land" and rooms for shared work. It became a cult text for builders — including, famously, software designers. A stuff library is a pattern in exactly this spirit: a small, repeatable structure that makes a neighborhood more livable. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22A%20Pattern%20Language%22%20au%3AAlexander).)_

**The Great Good Place — Ray Oldenburg (1989)**
Oldenburg named the "third place" — not home, not work, but the café, barbershop, or corner tavern where community actually happens, where regulars are made and strangers become neighbors. His warning was that America was designing these places out of existence. A lending library's pickup porch is a third place with a cordless drill on it. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22The%20Great%20Good%20Place%22%20au%3AOldenburg).)_

**Bowling Alone: The Collapse and Revival of American Community — Robert D. Putnam (2000; from a 1995 essay)**
The data-heavy landmark on the decline of American social capital — fewer leagues, lodges, dinners, and favors — and what that costs us in health, safety, and democracy. Putnam distinguished "bonding" from "bridging" ties; borrowing across a neighborhood builds the rarer, bridging kind. This is the problem statement to which a stuff library is one small answer. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Bowling%20Alone%22%20au%3APutnam).)_

**Palaces for the People: How Social Infrastructure Can Help Fight Inequality, Polarization, and the Decline of Civic Life — Eric Klinenberg (2018)** ⭐ start here
Klinenberg's name for libraries, parks, and community gardens — "social infrastructure" — captures the idea that shared physical places do the invisible work of holding society together, and even determine who survives a heat wave. He treats the public library as the great undervalued institution of our time. A neighborhood stuff library is his argument, extended to everything with a handle. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Palaces%20for%20the%20People%22%20au%3AKlinenberg).)_

## 6. The sharing economy & its cautionary tales

**What's Mine Is Yours: The Rise of Collaborative Consumption — Rachel Botsman & Roo Rogers (2010)** ⭐ start here
The optimistic opening bell of the sharing decade: idle stuff plus internet trust equals access over ownership, from car-sharing to tool libraries. The power drill used "twelve to thirteen minutes in its entire lifetime" became the movement's favorite statistic. Read it for the original promise — then read Slee for what venture capital did with it. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22What%27s%20Mine%20Is%20Yours%22%20au%3ABotsman).)_

**What's Yours Is Mine: Against the Sharing Economy — Tom Slee (2015)**
The sharp-eyed rebuttal: "sharing" platforms took the warm language of community and commons and used it to build extractive, deregulated marketplaces — Uber and Airbnb wearing Kropotkin's clothes. Slee's book is why a genuinely non-commercial, open-source lending platform has to say so out loud. Cautionary reading, in the most useful sense. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22What%27s%20Yours%20Is%20Mine%22%20au%3ASlee).)_

**Ours to Hack and to Own: The Rise of Platform Cooperativism — Trebor Scholz & Nathan Schneider, eds. (2017)**
Forty-some short pieces asking the obvious next question: if platforms are the new infrastructure, why don't the people who use them own them? Platform cooperativism — democratic ownership plus open technology — is the sharing economy with its original values restored. An AGPL neighborhood lending platform is a cousin of everything in this book. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22Ours%20to%20Hack%20and%20to%20Own%22%20au%3AScholz).)_

**After the Gig: How the Sharing Economy Got Hijacked and How to Win It Back — Juliet Schor (2020)**
Schor and her team spent a decade inside the sharing economy — gig apps, but also time banks, makerspaces, and food swaps — and reported honestly on both the hijacking and the survivors. Her finding that nonprofit, community-governed sharing initiatives behave differently from VC-backed ones is the closest thing to an evidence base for doing it the StuffLibrary way. _([Find it at your library](https://search.worldcat.org/search?q=ti%3A%22After%20the%20Gig%22%20au%3ASchor).)_

## The ideas, alive today

These aren't just books — the same energy runs through living organizations you can join right now:

- **Shareable** — nonprofit newsroom and action hub for the sharing/solidarity economy; publishers of countless "how to start a tool library" guides. https://www.shareable.net
- **The tool library movement** — hundreds of volunteer-run lending libraries worldwide, from Berkeley's (lending since 1979) onward; the living proof of concept. _(See [our directory of lending libraries by state](/lending-libraries).)_
- **Library of Things (UK)** — borrow-anything kiosks in London and beyond; a working model of "usefulness over ownership" at city scale. https://www.libraryofthings.co.uk
- **Repair Café International** — the global network of free, volunteer-run repair meetups (thousands of cafés since 2009) keeping stuff alive and neighbors acquainted. https://www.repaircafe.org/en/
- **The Repair Association / Right to Repair** — the coalition winning laws that let owners and communities fix what they own. https://www.repair.org (see also iFixit's free repair manuals: https://www.ifixit.com)
- **Buy Nothing Project** — hyperlocal gift-economy groups where neighbors give, lend, and ask freely. https://buynothingproject.org _(See [our field guide to the online sharing ecosystem](/sharing-world).)_
- **P2P Foundation** — long-running research commons on peer production, commoning, and platform cooperativism. https://p2pfoundation.net
- **Ellen MacArthur Foundation** — the flagship circular-economy organization working with business, cities, and schools. https://www.ellenmacarthurfoundation.org
