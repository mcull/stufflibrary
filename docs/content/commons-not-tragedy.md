# The Tragedy of the Commons Is Optional

> Draft page content for stufflibrary.org — July 2026.
> Working title options: "The Tragedy of the Commons Is Optional" / "Why We Trust Neighbors (It's Not Optimism)" / "Built on Evidence, Not Vibes"
> Tone target: plainspoken, grounded, a little dry. Not professorial, not starry-eyed.

## Page content

### The story everyone knows

In 1968, an ecologist named Garrett Hardin published an essay in _Science_ called "The Tragedy of the Commons." It asked readers to picture a pasture open to all: each herdsman keeps adding cattle, because he gets the whole benefit of each cow while the whole village shares the cost of the overgrazing. "Ruin is the destination toward which all men rush," Hardin wrote. "Freedom in a commons brings ruin to all."

It became one of the most cited papers ever written, and it hardened into common sense: people can't share things without a corporation or a government making them behave. If you've ever hesitated to lend a ladder to a neighbor, some version of this story was probably whispering in your ear.

Here's the thing, though. Hardin's pasture was a thought experiment. Nobody in it talks to anyone. There are no rules, no membership, no reputations, no Thursday-morning meetings — no _neighbors_, really, just strangers with cattle. Even Hardin eventually conceded the point, writing in 1991 that his essay should have been titled "The Tragedy of the _Unmanaged_ Commons." And historians who went back and looked found that the actual English commons his metaphor leaned on were carefully regulated for centuries — membership was limited, grazing was capped per household, and village courts enforced the rules. The commons didn't die of overgrazing. They died of enclosure — of being fenced off and privatized.

### The researcher who checked

Elinor Ostrom was a political scientist who did something unusual: instead of theorizing about whether communities could manage shared resources, she went and looked. For decades, she and her colleagues at Indiana University collected and compared thousands of real cases — field studies by anthropologists, historians, and engineers — plus her own fieldwork, from Los Angeles groundwater basins to Nepali irrigation canals.

What she found: ordinary communities all over the world have governed shared resources successfully, some for many centuries. Not because the people were saints. Because they'd worked out durable arrangements — and the durable ones, it turned out, kept sharing certain features.

A few of her cases, because they're worth knowing:

- **Törbel, Switzerland.** A village in the Alps has managed shared summer pastures, forests, and irrigation with written rules going back to 1224. A regulation from 1517 — still enforced when researchers visited in the 20th century — says no family may send more cows up the mountain than it can feed through the winter. Simple, self-limiting, easy to check: the cows get counted anyway, because the count determines each family's share of the cheese.
- **Valencia, Spain.** Farmers on the irrigation canals around Valencia have settled water disputes for centuries in the Tribunal de las Aguas — a court of elected canal representatives that meets Thursday mornings outside the cathedral, hears both sides in public, without lawyers, and rules on the spot. It still meets today.
- **Japan's iriai lands.** During the Tokugawa era, some twelve million hectares of forest and mountain meadow were managed in common by thousands of villages. Villages hired patrols, and penalties were deliberately graduated: a first minor offense might cost you some cash and a round of sake for the inspectors; only serious, repeated violations escalated. The researcher who studied these villages reported she had not found a single commons that suffered ecological destruction while it was still a commons.
- **The zanjeras of the Philippines.** Hundreds of farmer-built irrigation societies in Ilocos Norte, where each member's fields are deliberately scattered along the canal — everyone farms both near the head and near the tail. So in a dry year, the fair fix is obvious to all: shut off the bottom section, which hurts everyone equally.
- **Maine lobster fisheries.** Maine's "harbor gangs" informally controlled who could fish where for generations, and developed conservation practices — like notching the tails of egg-bearing females so no one can ever legally sell them — that the state later wrote into law. The fishery thrived for decades while others up and down the coast collapsed.
- **Nepal's farmer-run canals.** Ostrom's team studied 229 irrigation systems in Nepal. The ones farmers governed themselves — with crude mud-and-brush dams — delivered more water, stayed in better repair, and grew more food than the concrete systems built and run by government agencies. The engineering was worse; the governance was better.

In 2009, Ostrom became the first woman to win the Nobel in economics, "for her analysis of economic governance, especially the commons."

She was careful about what the evidence did and didn't show. Unmanaged commons really do collapse — plenty of fisheries and aquifers have. Her point was that tragedy is a possible outcome, not a law of nature. And she resisted turning her findings into a formula. "No panaceas!" was practically her motto. But across the long-lived commons, she documented eight recurring design principles — the things the survivors had in common that the failures lacked.

### What this has to do with borrowing a drill

StuffLibrary is a commons — a small one. The shared resource isn't a pasture or a fishery; it's a neighborhood's pool of ladders, stand mixers, and post-hole diggers, plus something more fragile: everyone's willingness to keep sharing.

So we built the platform around Ostrom's eight principles. Not as decoration — as architecture. The claim here is modest: software can't make a community care for a commons. What it can do is make the healthy patterns the default instead of an accident.

1. **Clear boundaries.** _Ostrom: everyone knows who's a member and what's being shared._ A StuffLibrary isn't "the internet borrowing your drill." It's a named library of neighbors, with an explicit join step, real names and faces, and a visible catalog. You can always see who's in and what's shared.

2. **Rules that fit the place.** _Ostrom: rules written for somewhere else won't work here._ A tool library in a dense apartment block and one on a rural road shouldn't run identical rules. Each library sets its own norms — loan lengths, expectations, house culture — rather than inheriting one global policy.

3. **The governed make the rules.** _Ostrom: people who live under the rules get a real say in changing them._ A library's norms belong to its members, not to us. The platform's defaults are starting points, not terms of service.

4. **Monitoring, by us — meaning everyone.** _Ostrom: someone keeps an eye on things, and that someone answers to the members — or is the members._ No surveillance apparatus; just shared visibility. Any member can see what's out, with whom, and when it's due. In Törbel, the cow count doubled as the cheese ledger. Here, the checkout card is the monitoring.

5. **Graduated consequences.** _Ostrom: first offenses get a nudge, not a hammer._ A late return dents your trust score a little, and the dent fades with time and good behavior. Lose something and the mark is bigger, but it too can heal. Durable commons almost never lead with expulsion — first they lead with sake for the inspectors, so to speak — and neither do we.

6. **Cheap, fast conflict resolution.** _Ostrom: a low-cost local way to sort out disagreements before they fester._ When something comes back damaged or doesn't come back, there's a simple, factual dispute record both sides can see — closer to Valencia's Thursday-morning court than to a legal process.

7. **The right to self-organize.** _Ostrom: outside authorities let the group run itself._ Each library admits its own members, sets its own rules, and settles its own disputes. The platform's job is to recognize that self-organization, not to override it. (This is also why the code is open source under AGPL — even _we_ can't enclose this commons.)

8. **Nested layers.** _Ostrom: small groups handle small problems and link up for bigger ones._ Your library handles its own day-to-day. But libraries sit inside a wider network — sharing norms that work, and reputations that travel with appropriate weight — the way Ostrom's Filipino irrigation societies federated to maintain the one dam none of them could rebuild alone.

### The short version

People have been sharing successfully — with rules, not vibes — since at least 1224. The failures Hardin imagined are real, but they're what happens to commons _without_ boundaries, monitoring, and gentle consequences, not to commons in general. We didn't invent any of this. We just read the research and built the defaults to match.
